I am a Ph.D. candidate in Electrical and Computer Engineering at Purdue University, where my research sits at the intersection of geometry, topology, and large-scale model optimization. I am driven by the challenge of making artificial intelligence more efficient, accessible, and interpretable—transforming high-performance models from "black boxes" into lean, explainable systems.

Research Focus
======
My research focuses on three core pillars of modern Machine Learning Engineering:
1. Model Compression & Efficiency: Developing training-free, zero-shot frameworks like TraceNAS to optimize LLM architecture (depth and width) across models ranging from 2B to 70B+ parameters.
2. Efficient Training via Topology: Leveraging differential geometry—specifically Forman-Ricci curvature and Grassmannian manifolds—to preserve manifold geometry during data pruning and model distillation.
3. Data Explainability & Interpretability: Using Sparse Auto Encoders (SAEs) and mechanistic interpretability to disentangle feature representations and identify memorization patterns within foundation models.

Academic & Professional Journey
======
Prior to my time at Purdue, I earned my Bachelor’s degree in Electronics and Communication Engineering from PES University and gained industry experience as an R&D intern at ABB India, where I developed predictive maintenance models using RNNs.

My recent research has been recognized at venues such as TMLR and NeurIPS Workshops covering diverse topics from Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) to robust dataset pruning.
